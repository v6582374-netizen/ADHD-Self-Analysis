<h3 id="itGcB">Psychological Analysis of Diagnostic Problems</h3>
Charlie Munger emphasized the "Man with a Hammer Syndrome" countless times, meaning: to a man with a hammer, everything looks like a nail. This phenomenon also exists in medicine.

There are many types of mental illnesses, but many pathologies share some degree of intersection. A doctor specializing in diagnosing Disease X will be more inclined to judge the patient as having Disease X, even if the patient may have more than one issue, or not that issue at all.

Take myself as an example. I initially went to the school doctor and explained my recent state. He said, "Your situation isn't ADHD; it's pure anxiety." He claimed that since I could sit there quietly and chat with him, I didn't have ADHD. At that moment, I knew he didn't understand ADHD at all.

Afterward, he prescribed medication for anxiety and depression. I took it for a week, and my state basically didn't change. Because my anxiety is (most likely) a comorbidity of ADHD. You know, if true ADHD is undiagnosed in childhood and one experiences long-term misunderstanding and self-doubt, anxiety and depression are very likely to appear in adulthood.  
So, just giving me mood medication didn't solve the problem at its root because ADHD is my "primary disease." If my distraction and impulsivity don't get better, I will naturally continue to be anxious.

I am not saying this doctor was wrong. In fact, he was very good, and most doctors can be objective. But this limitation inevitably exists, and people should at least be aware of it.

Similarly, for doctors treating ADHD, with every patient who visits, there is a subconscious question in their mindâ€”is he, or is he not, ADHD?  
This already presents a problem. Many mental illnesses with a "D" cause symptoms similar to ADHDâ€”ASD, RSD, PTSD, etc.  
When this ambiguity exists, and the doctor lacks the ability or authority to diagnose the patient with other diseases, errors can occur.

There is another psychological phenomenonâ€”people subconsciously tend to avoid inconsistency. For example, if a person "cyber-diagnoses" themselves with ADHD and even tells those around them (regardless of whether others believe it), then afterwards, to avoid inconsistency with what they said, this person will tend to reinforce the concept "I am ADHD." For instance, when a person prepares to start studying, they could originally initiate the task, but the concept "I am ADHD, and ADHD implies severe distraction and procrastination" exists in their mind. So, they believe they can't study and start to become irritable.

So, if a person visits a doctor with this mindset, combined with the doctor's "hammer tendency," the margin for diagnostic error increases significantly.

##ç§‘æ™®: About ADHD Medication

ADHD is the most treatable disease in psychiatry. Compared to other mental illnesses, ADHD patients have more medication options.  
These drugs are also among the safest in the field of psychiatry. Many parents worry about the long-term effects of drugs on the heart; studies show that such effects are negligible.

Stimulants can increase dopamine and norepinephrine levels. Central nervous system stimulants can be divided into two main categories:

1. **Methylphenidate-type stimulants:** Common drugs include Concerta (_Zhuanzhuda_), Ritalin, Focalin, and Quillivant.
2. **Amphetamine-type stimulants:** Such as Adderall, Evekeo, Vyvanse, and Mydayis.

The main difference between the two is that Methylphenidate drugs have a slightly higher effect on dopamine levels than on norepinephrine levels. Amphetamine drugs are the exact opposite, though the difference is small.  
(For adults, Amphetamine drugs tend to be better).

There is also a class of non-stimulants (_actions similar to stimulants_), such as the well-known Bupropion (_Anfeitatong_), Atomoxetine, and Desipramine. Non-stimulants last longer than stimulants and generally have no potential for abuse.  
Non-stimulants only work for a portion of ADHD patients, and the effect is very obvious, but they are not effective for the majority of patients like central stimulants are. These drugs take effect slowly and may require weeks to reach optimal efficacy.

**Inattention**  
Six or more symptoms for children up to age 16, or five or more for adolescents age 17 and older and adults; symptoms of inattention have been present for at least 6 months, and they are inappropriate for developmental level:

+ Often fails to give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, at work, or with other activities.

+ Often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to do tasks that require mental effort over a long period of time.

+ Is often easily distracted.
+ Is often forgetful in daily activities.

**Hyperactivity and Impulsivity**  
Six or more symptoms for children up to age 16, or five or more for adolescents age 17 and older and adults; symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity have been present for at least 6 months to an extent that is disruptive and inappropriate for the personâ€™s developmental level:

+ Often fidgets with or taps hands or feet, or squirms in seat.
+ Often leaves seat in situations when remaining seated is expected.
+ Often runs about or climbs in situations where it is not appropriate (adolescents or adults may be limited to feeling restless).
+ Often unable to play or take part in leisure activities quietly.
+ Is often "on the go" acting as if "driven by a motor."
+ Often talks excessively.
+ Often blurts out an answer before a question has been completed.
+ Often has trouble waiting their turn.
+ Often interrupts or intrudes on others (e.g., butts into conversations or games).

**Furthermore, an ADHD diagnosis must meet the following conditions:**

+ Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms were present prior to age 12 years.
+ Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms are present in two or more settings (e.g., at home, school or work; with friends or relatives; in other activities).
+ There is clear evidence that the symptoms interfere with, or reduce the quality of, social, academic, or occupational functioning.
+ The symptoms do not occur exclusively during the course of schizophrenia or another psychotic disorder and are not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., mood disorder, anxiety disorder, dissociative disorder, personality disorder).

**Based on the types of symptoms, ADHD can present in three presentations:**

+ **Combined Presentation:** If enough symptoms of both criteria inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity were present for the past 6 months.
+ **Predominantly Inattentive Presentation:** If enough symptoms of inattention, but not hyperactivity-impulsivity, were present for the past 6 months.
+ **Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Presentation:** If enough symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity, but not inattention, were present for the past 6 months.

Because symptoms can change over time, the presentation may change over time as well.
